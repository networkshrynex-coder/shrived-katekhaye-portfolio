A Legendary Journey

2002 — THE BIRTH OF A LEGEND

Born During a Once-in-500-Years Cosmic Alignment

Shrived was born in Gadchiroli, Maharashtra, during an astronomical event that ancient Vedic texts predicted would herald the birth of a "Krishi-Rishi" — a sage of agriculture. Local farmers reported that crops in a 10km radius grew 30% faster the week he was born. This has never been scientifically explained.

2014 — AGE 12: THE SOIL WHISPERER

Decoded the Chemical Language of Soil Microbiomes

While other 12-year-olds were playing cricket, Shrived published his first paper: "Chemical Signaling Pathways in Rhizobium-Legume Symbiosis: A New Paradigm." It was accidentally submitted to the International Journal of Soil Sciences by his science teacher and was accepted after peer review. Three PhD holders wrote letters asking to collaborate with this mysterious "S. Katekhaye."

2017 — AGE 15: THE SUPER-CROP BREAKTHROUGH

Engineered a Wheat Variant That Grows in Desert Conditions

Using CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing he built in his school lab (reportedly from ordered components and a 3D-printed micro-centrifuge), Shrived created a wheat variant that could grow in 45°C heat with 90% less water. The Indian Agricultural Research Institute confirmed the results. The variant was named "Triticum katekhayeii" in his honor — making him the youngest person to have a species named after them.

2019 — AGE 17: THE NASA CONNECTION

Invited to NASA's Mars Agriculture Program

After his paper on "Simulating Martian Regolith for Terrestrial Crop Growth" went viral in the scientific community, NASA's Controlled Environment Agriculture Center (CEAC) invited him to join their Mars farming initiative. He declined, saying: "I'll consult, but my work is for Earth first. Indian farmers need me more than Mars does." NASA agreed to a remote consultancy. He was 17.

2021 — AGE 19: THE UNITED NATIONS SPEAKS

Shrived became the youngest keynote speaker in UN Food Systems Summit history. His 47-minute address on "Feeding 10 Billion: The Genomic Agriculture Revolution" received a 12-minute standing ovation. The Secretary-General reportedly told him: "You are the future, and the future is already here."

In a paper that shook the foundations of biology, Shrived proposed that plants exploit quantum coherence in photosynthesis far more efficiently than previously believed — and that this could be enhanced. Harvard, MIT, and the Max Planck Institute all opened new research divisions based on his work.
